# Longform format (blog / Substack / Medium)

## Structure
1. **Title** — specific, not clickbait. Promise a concrete payoff.
2. **Lede** (first paragraph) — earn the scroll. State stakes, hook with tension or specificity.
3. **Thesis** — one sentence, early (within first 200 words).
4. **Body** — supporting sections, each advancing the thesis.
5. **Pushback / nuance** — address the obvious counter.
6. **Synthesis** — what follows from all of this.
7. **Close** — what the reader should do / think / consider.

## Length
- 800-1500 words: tight, focused (default for Substack)
- 1500-3000 words: deep essay, must earn every word
- > 3000 words: almost always too long, split or cut

## Section headers
- Use H2 for main sections, H3 for sub
- Headers should be scannable — they make sense read alone
- Don't over-fragment (< 150 words per section = probably bullets in disguise)

## Voice calibration
- **Write sentences, not bullets.** Bullets in longform signal "I didn't want to think about flow."
- Use bullets only for: enumerated parallel things, step-by-step instructions, comparison.
- One-sentence paragraphs are allowed. Use for emphasis, sparingly.
- Vary sentence length: short punch after 2-3 longer ones.

## Evidence
- Lead with the strongest argument, not the longest
- Each section needs at least one of: example, number, quote, named reference
- Hyperlink sources inline — don't make readers hunt

## Hook patterns (lede)
- **Scene**: "It's 2am. <specific sensory detail>. I realize..."
- **Paradox**: "The thing everyone believes about X is wrong. And the reason is weirder than you'd think."
- **Stakes**: "If you're <demographic>, this will cost you <amount/time/opportunity>."
- **Question that won't leave you**: "Why did <surprising outcome> happen?"

## Thai longform
- Paragraphs can be slightly shorter than English (Thai density is higher)
- Technical terms stay in English
- Sentence structure: avoid long nested clauses — break into separate sentences
- Tone: conversational > formal for Substack/blog

## Common failure modes to avoid
- **Bury the lede** — thesis arrives at word 800
- **Academic hedging** — "it could be argued that" — just say it
- **Listicle disguised as essay** — if every section is "5 reasons / 3 things", it's a list
- **No position** — "here are some perspectives" without picking one
- **Soft close** — "so, what do you think?" — have a stronger ending

## Publication checklist (writer fills in self-critique section)
- [ ] Could I cut 20% without losing anything?
- [ ] Does the lede work for someone who's never heard of me?
- [ ] Does every section earn its space?
- [ ] Is the thesis clear by word 200?
- [ ] Would I send this to the smartest person I know?
